Periodic Steady-State Control of a Handkerchief-Spinning Task
Using a Parallel Anti-Parallelogram Tendon-driven Wrist

Abstract

Spinning flexible objects, exemplified by traditional Chinese handkerchief performances, demands periodic steady-state motions under nonlinear dynamics with frictional contacts and boundary constraints. Humans learn this skill intuitively, whereas robots lack suitable wrist dexterity and effective strategies. We propose a parallel anti-parallelogram (PAP) tendon-driven wrist with rapid dynamic response and a control-oriented model for initiation dynamics. Optimized initiation strategies enable robust transitions from rest to stable spinning. Our results show that combining PAP wrist dexterity with model-based control equips humanoid robots with new capabilities for manipulating flexible objects in dynamic tasks.

Overview

Contributions

  • Parallel anti-parallelogram tendon-driven wrist: high dynamic response, $90^\circ$ omnidirectional rotation, shortened effective length.
  • Control-oriented modeling and initiation strategy: robust rest-to-steady transitions without explicit friction modeling.
  • Hierarchical control: two-level controller with kinematic initiation and motor-level periodic maintenance.
  • Empirical validation: simulations and hardware confirm robust spin initiation and sustained periodic motion.

Experimental Results

Performance of Different Strategies

We compare acceleration-based, directional-throw, and periodic-energy strategies. The acceleration-based strategy unfolds the handkerchief quickly and achieves stable spinning, consistent with simulation analysis.
